# Mancala: Rules, Setup, and Strategy Guide

## Introduction

Mancala is one of the oldest known board games in the world, with origins tracing back thousands of years across Africa and Asia. Despite its ancient roots, it remains a deeply engaging two-player strategy game that rewards careful planning and forward thinking. The most widely played Western variant — often simply called *Mancala* or *Kalah* — is easy to learn but offers surprising strategic depth.

---

## The Board

A standard Mancala board consists of:

- **Two rows of six small pits** (also called holes or cups), one row per player
- **Two large pits** called *stores* (or *mancalas*), one at each end of the board

Each player owns the row of six pits closest to them, and the store to their right.

```
   <-- Player 2's side -->
  [6][5][4][3][2][1]
[B]                  [A]
  [1][2][3][4][5][6]
   <-- Player 1's side -->
```

- **Store A** belongs to Player 1 (on their right)
- **Store B** belongs to Player 2 (on their right)

---

## Setup

At the start of the game, place **4 stones** (seeds, beads, or pebbles) in each of the 12 small pits. Both stores begin empty.

```
   [4][4][4][4][4][4]
[0]                  [0]
   [4][4][4][4][4][4]
```

## How to Play

### Taking a Turn

On your turn, choose any one of the six pits on your side of the board that contains at least one stone. Pick up all the stones in that pit and **sow** them one by one, dropping a single stone into each pit moving counter-clockwise around the board.

Stones are dropped into:
- Each small pit on your side as you pass
- Your own store (when you reach it)
- Each small pit on your opponent's side as you continue
- **Skip your opponent's store** — never drop a stone there

Continue sowing until all the picked-up stones have been placed.

### Earning an Extra Turn

If the **last stone you place lands in your own store**, you earn a free bonus turn. You may take as many bonus turns in a row as this rule allows.

### Capturing Stones

If the **last stone you place lands in an empty pit on your own side**, and the pit directly opposite on your opponent's side contains stones, you **capture** both your landing stone and all the stones in the opposite pit. Place all captured stones in your store.

If the opposite pit is empty, no capture occurs — your stone simply remains in the empty pit.

---

## Ending the Game

The game ends when **all six pits on one player's side are empty**. When this happens:

- The player whose side still has stones **collects all remaining stones** on their side and adds them to their own store.
- Both players count the stones in their stores.
- The player with the **most stones wins**. There are 48 stones total, so a score above 24 wins.

## Strategy Guide

### 1. Aim for Extra Turns

Extra turns are one of the most powerful advantages in Mancala. Count the stones in each pit on your side and identify which ones end in your store. Prioritising these moves lets you take multiple turns in a row, building momentum and denying your opponent tempo.

As a quick rule of thumb, a pit that is **N pits away from your store** contains exactly **N stones** if it will land in your store. For example, the pit immediately to the left of your store (pit 6) needs 1 stone to earn a bonus; pit 5 needs 2 stones; pit 1 needs 6 stones.

### 2. Set Up Chain Turns

Look for sequences of moves that each earn an extra turn. A single well-planned chain can let you sow several times before your opponent gets to move, dramatically shifting the stone count in your favour. Spotting two or three-turn chains is the hallmark of an intermediate player.

### 3. Protect the Right Side (Pit 6)

The pit immediately adjacent to your store is strategically important. If it fills up with many stones, a single move can sow a large number of pits, including your opponent's side — which is usually wasteful. Conversely, keeping this pit at 1 stone is a reliable source of bonus turns. Be mindful of how stones accumulate there.

### 4. Use Captures Wisely

Captures can swing the game quickly, but don't pursue them blindly. Before landing in an empty pit on your side, check two things: whether the opposite pit actually contains stones, and whether taking that capture is more valuable than other available moves. A capture of 1 or 2 stones may be less valuable than a move that earns an extra turn.

Equally, **watch for your opponent's capture threats** and keep vulnerable pits on your side stocked with stones to prevent empty-pit landings.

### 5. Play the Offensive in the Early Game

In the opening turns, prioritise getting stones into your store quickly. Moves from pits 3–6 (counting from the store end) tend to reward extra turns or good positioning early on. Avoid leaving your pits depleted on one side of the board, as this limits your future options.

### 6. Manage Stone Distribution

Having many pits with stones gives you more options each turn. Spreading stones across your pits (rather than concentrating them in one or two) keeps your moves flexible. A common mistake is emptying pits on one side of your row, leaving yourself with fewer choices later in the game.

### 7. Starve Your Opponent

As the endgame approaches, try to empty **your own pits** before your opponent empties theirs. The player who empties their side last gets to collect all remaining stones on the board — a potentially large bonus. If you are ahead, timing this correctly can lock in a win; if you are behind, forcing the end of the game quickly can save you from a larger defeat.

### 8. Count the Stones

Strong players keep a rough mental count of the stones in both stores. Knowing whether you are ahead or behind helps you decide whether to play aggressively (extending the game to build up your store) or defensively (ending the game before your opponent catches up).

## Quick Reference

| Situation | Key Action |
|---|---|
| Last stone lands in your store | Take another turn |
| Last stone lands in empty pit on your side | Capture opposite pit's stones |
| Last stone lands in empty pit on opponent's side | No capture, turn ends |
| One side empties completely | Opponent collects all remaining stones |
| Tie (24–24) | Draw |

## Tips for New Players

- **Start simple:** Play a few games without worrying about strategy. Get comfortable with the sowing direction and the basic rules before planning ahead.
- **Count before you move:** Before picking up a pit's stones, count how many there are and trace where they will land. A moment's thought prevents many mistakes.
- **Watch your opponent:** Pay attention to which of their pits could earn them bonus turns or captures next move, and try to disrupt these when possible.
- **Have fun:** Mancala games tend to be short (10–20 minutes), making it easy to replay and experiment with different approaches.
